## Lower link line — not the same “angled” as the upper

**Geometry (absolute; keys in `roll-centre.md`):** In **side view**, a **more acute** line from the inner-lower to outer-lower pick-ups (smaller `under_lower_arm` and `under_hub` stacks, all else equal) **lowers roll centre** on that end. **Larger** inner-lower and outer-lower (hub) stacks **raise roll centre** and read as a **less acute (flatter) lower line**. The upper link’s “**flatter** / **more angled**” map is different: a **more angled upper** in the `roll-centre` sense **pairs with higher RC**; a **more acute lower line** **pairs with lower RC**. Do not describe both arms with the same “more angled” shortcut.

## Camber in roll (camber gain)

**Geometry (absolute):** **Camber in roll** (how much the wheel **adds negative camber** as the suspension works in a corner) is set by the link and **roll centre** package on that end, not by static `camber_front` / `camber_rear` alone.

- A **flatter** upper link in the `roll-centre` **Flatter vs more angled** sense **decreases camber gain** in roll on that end.
- A **more angled** upper link in that same definition **increases camber gain** in roll on that end.
- **Higher** roll centre on that end from **inner** **lower arm** and **outer lower** (hub) shims, as in the **Lower arm** block in `roll-centre`, **increases** camber gain in roll in combination with the upper-link state.

**On-track (subjective):** **Lower** camber gain from a flatter upper is one reason a **sustained-grip** setup can show **less initial bite**; full feel context stays in `response-vs-sustained-grip.md` and `balance-and-grip.md`.
