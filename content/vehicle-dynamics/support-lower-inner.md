## Support — inner lower arm vs springs and ARB

**Support** here means how firmly geometry carries lateral load and transfer—not only **roll centre** height, but **how forces run from the tire into the chassis**. The **inner lower pickup** (**under lower arm** shims, keys `under_lower_arm_shims_*`) is often the strongest lever for that **fundamental** character: it changes RC **and** the stiffness of the **kinematic** response. **Springs** and **ARB** change roll resistance too, but they do not scale the same way with cornering force as moving the **lower arm** geometry.

## Raising the lower arm — RC, support, and load

**Raising** the inner lower stack **raises roll centre** on that corner and adds **support**: the car feels **stiffer**, more held up at the limit. That extra support tends to track **load**—as grip and forces go up, geometric support rises with them. Tuning only **spring** or **ARB** can mask a lack of support until **grip or pace** increases; then you may chase the same problem again. When you need a durable change to how the car **supports** itself, **inner lower arm** geometry is often the right place to work—not as a substitute for dampers, but as the **base** balance.

## Rear lower arm too low — soft at the limit

When **rear** inner lower is **too low**, the car often feels **soft at the limit**: the rear never quite **platforms** under power or in sustained lateral load. You can add **spring**, **ARB**, or damping to stiffen feel, but if **grip** or **forces** step up, you can end up fighting for **support** repeatedly. **Raising** the **rear** lower inner pickup addresses **rear support** at the source. Much of this “support” language in practice refers especially to the **rear** axle.

## Rear lower arm too high — over-support

If the **rear** lower arm is **too high**, you can have **too much support**: the car feels **stiff** and **snappy**, rolls little, and **mid-corner** the **rear** may **lose grip** or feel nervous—it will not **breathe** in roll the way a balanced car does. The opposite problem to “too soft”: you are **over-supported** geometrically. **Lowering** the rear inner lower stack (within sensible range) reduces that geometric stiffness and can restore roll and rear grip mid-corner—then re-trim spring/ARB.

## Front inner lower arm — support and handling (theory)

**Raising** the **front** inner lower pickup **raises front roll centre** and adds **geometric support at the front** in the same sense as elsewhere: how lateral load is carried **kinematically**, not only via spring and ARB. Much casual **“support”** talk still centres on the **rear** (platform, throttle, fishtail)—but a **front** under–lower-arm change is still a **real** support + RC move; describe it as **front** support when the diff is **front** keys, not by borrowing **rear-only** stories.

**Reported tendencies** (tires, grip, and balance-dependent—**test on track**): more **initial** bite or **reactive** turn-in can come with **higher** front RC; **mid-corner** and **exit** some drivers feel **less front grip** or **more push / understeer** than with a **lower** front RC or a **flatter** front upper-link balance. **Bumps** may feel **less planted** or **more slide** when that end is **stiffer in roll**. Some drivers find the car **easier to drive** overall (more forgiving or less peaky) even if one phase feels weaker—preference varies. Combine with **initial-vs-overall-grip** and **per-end** upper-link theory in `roll-centre.md` when **front** inner lower moves with upper link.
