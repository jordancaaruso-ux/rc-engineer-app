## Spring rate (general)

**Keys (examples):** `spring_front`, `spring_rear`, `spring_gap_front`, `spring_gap_rear`, `front_spring_rate_gf_mm`, `rear_spring_rate_gf_mm` — your sheet may use **preset springs**, **gap**, or **rate (gf/mm)**; they all change **effective spring rate** at that end.

**Higher spring rates** generally give **more initial grip**, a **quicker-reacting** car, **more initial steering**, and **more initial rear grip** — until the tire is overloaded. **Surface grip, speed, and damping** change what “stiff enough” means.

## Front spring (stiffer vs softer)

**Stiffer front spring** often behaves **similar to a stiffer front ARB** in feel: **more initial steering** and a **more reactive** front end, but often **less mid-corner steering** at **low speeds / low lateral load** (e.g. tight hairpins). At **high speed / high load**, a stiffer front can feel like **more steering** in general because the platform stays flatter. **Net:** many drivers report **less mid-corner rotation / oversteer** when they go too stiff on the front. **Softer front spring:** **less initial steering**, often **more hairpin / mid-corner steering**; can feel **easier to drive**. **Too soft** can feel **imprecise** or vague; **too stiff** can feel **over-reactive** and **hard to drive**.

## Rear spring (stiffer vs softer)

**Stiffer rear spring** often **reduces mid-corner rotation**; can **improve on-throttle steering** (or **rear stability**) at **high load** depending on balance. Many drivers feel the car **squares up** on exits and **wants to go straight** sooner. **Too soft** can feel **vague** and not want to **drive straight off** the corner. **Stiffer rear** often feels like **more rear grip** in many conditions but **less compliant** and sometimes **harder to drive**. **Softer rear** can sometimes feel like **more overall rear grip** — **it is not always predictable** across grip levels, bumps, and layout; **test** and tune with **dampers and ARB** together.
